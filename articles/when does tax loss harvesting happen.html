Introduction

Tax loss harvesting is a strategy used by investors to reduce their tax liability. It involves selling investments that have lost value in order to offset any capital gains that may have been realized during the year. This strategy can be used to reduce the amount of taxes owed on investments, as well as to create a tax-free income stream.

Tax loss harvesting is a way for investors to take advantage of losses in their portfolio and use them to offset any gains they may have realized during the year. By doing this, investors can reduce their overall tax liability and potentially increase their after-tax returns. The strategy can also be used to create a tax-free income stream, as any losses realized can be used to offset any gains that may have been realized during the year.

When Does Tax Loss Harvesting Happen?

Tax loss harvesting typically happens at the end of the year when investors are preparing their taxes. Investors will look at their portfolio and identify any investments that have lost value since they were purchased. These investments can then be sold in order to realize a capital loss, which can then be used to offset any capital gains that may have been realized during the year.

It is important for investors to keep track of when they purchased each investment, as this will determine whether or not it qualifies for tax loss harvesting. Generally speaking, investments must be held for more than one year in order for losses on them to qualify for tax loss harvesting purposes.

In addition, it is important for investors to keep track of when they sell each investment in order to ensure that they are taking advantage of all available opportunities for tax loss harvesting. How Can Tax Loss Harvesting Help Investors?

Tax loss harvesting can help investors reduce their overall tax liability by allowing them to offset any capital gains with losses from other investments in their portfolio. This strategy can also help investors create a tax-free income stream by allowing them to use losses from one investment in order to offset gains from another investment without having to pay taxes on either transaction.
