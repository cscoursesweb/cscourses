What Is Cryptocurrency?

Before we dive into the specifics of whether or not your cryptocurrency losses are tax deductible, let’s take a moment to define what exactly cryptocurrency is. Cryptocurrency is a digital asset that uses cryptography to secure transactions and control the creation of new units of currency. It operates independently from any central bank or government authority, making it decentralized and largely unregulated.

Cryptocurrency can be used as an investment vehicle as well as a medium of exchange for goods and services. It can also be used to purchase other cryptocurrencies or fiat currencies such as US dollars or euros.

How Are Cryptocurrency Losses Taxed?

When it comes to taxes, there are two main ways that your cryptocurrency losses can be treated: as capital gains or ordinary income. Capital gains refer to profits made from selling an asset for more than its original purchase price; conversely, capital losses refer to losses incurred when selling an asset for less than its original purchase price.

If you acquired your cryptocurrency through mining (i.e., using computing power to solve complex mathematical problems in order to create new units of currency), then any profits made from selling that currency would be considered ordinary income and taxed accordingly. However, if you purchased your cryptocurrency through an exchange (i.e., buying it with fiat currency), then any profits made from selling that currency would be considered capital gains and taxed accordingly.

Now that we’ve discussed how your cryptocurrency losses may be taxed, let’s answer the question: Are they tax deductible? The answer is yes – but only under certain circumstances. If you purchased your cryptocurrency through an exchange (i.e., buying it with fiat currency) then any losses incurred when selling that currency would be considered capital losses and could potentially be deducted from your taxable income on your annual tax return (up to $3,000). However, if you acquired your cryptocurrency through mining (i.e., using computing power to solve complex mathematical problems in order to create new units of currency), then any losses incurred when selling that currency would not be considered capital losses and therefore would not be eligible for deduction on your annual tax return – they would instead be treated as ordinary income and taxed accordingly at whatever rate applies based on your total taxable income for the year in question..
