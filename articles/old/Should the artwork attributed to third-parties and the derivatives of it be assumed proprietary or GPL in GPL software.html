Should the artwork attributed to third-parties and the derivatives of it be assumed proprietary or GPL in GPL software?

The question of whether artwork attributed to third-parties and the derivatives of it should be assumed proprietary or GPL in GPL software is a complex one. On the one hand, the GPL is a license that grants users the freedom to use, modify, and redistribute software, and artwork is often considered part of the software. On the other hand, artwork is often created by third-parties, and the copyright of the artwork may be held by the artist or another entity.


In general, artwork that is attributed to third-parties and the derivatives of it should be assumed proprietary in GPL software. This is because the GPL does not grant users the right to use, modify, or redistribute artwork that is not owned by the software's copyright holder. The GPL only grants users the right to use, modify, and redistribute the software itself, not any artwork that is included with the software.


However, there are some exceptions to this rule. If the artwork is licensed under a compatible license, such as the Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike license, then it can be used, modified, and redistributed in GPL software. Additionally, if the artwork is in the public domain, then it can be used, modified, and redistributed in GPL software.
