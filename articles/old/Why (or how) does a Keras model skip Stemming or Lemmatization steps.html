Why (or how) does a Keras model skip Stemming or Lemmatization steps?

Keras is a powerful open-source deep learning library written in Python. It is used for fast prototyping, advanced research, and production. Keras models are used for a variety of tasks, including natural language processing (NLP). In NLP, stemming and lemmatization are two important pre-processing steps that are used to reduce the complexity of a text by normalizing words to their base forms.


Stemming and lemmatization are important steps in NLP because they help reduce the complexity of a text by normalizing words to their base forms. For example, the words “run”, “running”, and “ran” all have the same stem, “run”. Similarly, the words “am”, “are”, and “is” all have the same lemma, “be”. Stemming and lemmatization are important because they help reduce the size of the vocabulary that a model needs to learn, which can improve the accuracy of the model.


So why does a Keras model skip the stemming and lemmatization steps? The answer is that Keras models are designed to be fast and efficient. Stemming and lemmatization are time-consuming processes that can slow down the training process. Furthermore, Keras models are designed to work with raw text, so they don’t need to be pre-processed.


Keras models are also designed to be flexible. They can be used for a variety of tasks, including text classification, sentiment analysis, and language translation. Stemming and lemmatization are not always necessary for these tasks, so Keras models can skip these steps if they are not needed.


Finally, Keras models are designed to be easy to use. Stemming and lemmatization can be difficult to implement, so Keras models can skip these steps to make the model easier to use.
