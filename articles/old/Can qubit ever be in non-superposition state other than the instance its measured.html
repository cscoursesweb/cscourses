Can qubit ever be in non-superposition state other than the instance its measured?

The answer to the question of whether a qubit can ever be in a non-superposition state other than the instance its measured is yes. A qubit is a quantum bit, which is the basic unit of quantum information. It is a two-state quantum system, meaning it can exist in either of two states, usually referred to as 0 and 1. A qubit can also exist in a superposition of these two states, meaning it is in a combination of both 0 and 1 at the same time. This is the basis of quantum computing, which allows for the manipulation of information in ways that are not possible with classical computing.


However, a qubit can also exist in a non-superposition state, meaning it is in either 0 or 1, but not both. This is known as a “collapsed” state, and it occurs when the qubit is measured. When a qubit is measured, the superposition of 0 and 1 collapses into either 0 or 1, depending on the outcome of the measurement.


But a qubit can also exist in a non-superposition state without being measured. This is known as a “stabilized” state, and it occurs when the qubit is subjected to a specific type of quantum operation known as a “stabilizer”. A stabilizer is a quantum operation that preserves the state of the qubit, meaning it does not cause the qubit to collapse into either 0 or 1. This allows the qubit to remain in a non-superposition state, even when it is not being measured.
