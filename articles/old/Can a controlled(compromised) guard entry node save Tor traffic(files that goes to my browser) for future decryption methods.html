Can a controlled(compromised) guard/entry node save Tor traffic(files that goes to my browser) for future decryption methods?

The short answer to the question of whether a compromised guard/entry node can save Tor traffic for future decryption methods is yes, it is possible. However, it is important to understand the risks associated with using a compromised guard/entry node and the potential implications of doing so.


When using the Tor network, users rely on a series of nodes, known as guard/entry nodes, to route their traffic. These nodes are responsible for encrypting and decrypting the traffic that passes through them. If one of these nodes is compromised, it is possible for an attacker to gain access to the traffic that passes through it. This means that the attacker could potentially save the traffic for future decryption methods.


The risks associated with using a compromised guard/entry node are significant. For starters, the attacker could potentially gain access to sensitive information that is being sent through the Tor network. This could include passwords, financial information, or other confidential data. Additionally, the attacker could use the saved traffic to launch targeted attacks against other users of the Tor network.


In addition to the risks associated with using a compromised guard/entry node, there are also potential legal implications. Depending on the jurisdiction, it may be illegal to use a compromised guard/entry node to save traffic for future decryption methods. Furthermore, if the attacker is able to gain access to sensitive information, they may be liable for any damages caused by their actions.
