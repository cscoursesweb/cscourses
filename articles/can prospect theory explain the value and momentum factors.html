What Are Value and Momentum Factors?

Value and momentum factors are two distinct investment strategies that have been used by investors for decades. Value investing involves buying stocks that are undervalued relative to their intrinsic value, while momentum investing involves buying stocks that have recently experienced strong price appreciation. Both strategies can be used to generate returns in the stock market, but they require different approaches.

Value investing is based on the idea that stocks with low prices relative to their intrinsic value will eventually rise in price as the market recognizes their true worth. This strategy requires investors to do extensive research into a company’s financials in order to determine its true worth. Momentum investing, on the other hand, relies on the idea that stocks with recent strong performance will continue to outperform in the future. This strategy requires investors to identify stocks with strong recent performance and then buy them before they experience a correction or reversal of fortune.

Can Prospect Theory Explain These Factors?

Prospect theory is an economic theory developed by Nobel Prize-winning economist Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky which states that people tend to make decisions based on perceived gains or losses rather than absolute gains or losses. This theory has been applied extensively in finance, particularly when it comes to understanding investor behavior.

In terms of value investing, prospect theory suggests that investors may be more likely to buy undervalued stocks if they perceive them as having potential for large gains rather than small losses. Similarly, when it comes to momentum investing, prospect theory suggests that investors may be more likely to buy stocks with recent strong performance if they perceive them as having potential for large gains rather than small losses. In other words, prospect theory can help explain why some investors may be drawn towards value or momentum investing strategies over others.
