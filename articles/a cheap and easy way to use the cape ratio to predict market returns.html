Introduction

The CAPE ratio, or cyclically adjusted price-to-earnings ratio, is a popular tool used by investors to predict future market returns. It is based on the idea that stock prices are cyclical and that long-term trends can be identified by looking at the average earnings of a company over a 10-year period. By using this ratio, investors can get an idea of how much they should expect to earn from their investments in the future.

However, many investors find it difficult to use the CAPE ratio due to its complexity and cost. Fortunately, there are some simple and inexpensive ways to use the CAPE ratio to predict market returns without breaking the bank. In this blog post, we will discuss how you can use the CAPE ratio on a budget and make informed decisions about your investments.

What is the CAPE Ratio?

The CAPE ratio is a measure of stock market valuation that takes into account both current and past earnings of a company over a 10-year period. It is calculated by dividing the current price of a stock by its average earnings over 10 years (the “CAPE”). The higher the number, the more expensive stocks are relative to their historical earnings. The lower the number, the cheaper stocks are relative to their historical earnings.

The idea behind using this metric is that it gives investors an indication of whether stocks are currently undervalued or overvalued relative to their long-term average earnings. This can help investors identify potential opportunities for buying or selling stocks at attractive prices.

How Can You Use The CAPE Ratio To Predict Market Returns?

The most common way to use the CAPE ratio is as an indicator of future market returns. By looking at how expensive or cheap stocks are relative to their historical earnings, investors can get an idea of what kind of returns they should expect in the future. Generally speaking, if stocks are trading at high levels relative to their historical earnings (i.e., high CAPE ratios), then investors should expect lower returns in the future than if stocks were trading at low levels relative to their historical earnings (i.e., low CAPE ratios).

It’s important to note that while this metric can be useful for predicting future market returns, it’s not foolproof and should not be used as a sole basis for making investment decisions. Instead, it should be used in conjunction with other metrics such as price/earnings ratios and dividend yields when making investment decisions.
