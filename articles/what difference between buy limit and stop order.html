Introduction

When it comes to trading stocks, there are a variety of different orders that can be placed. Two of the most common types of orders are buy limit and stop order. While both of these orders can be used to purchase stocks, they have different purposes and should be used in different situations. In this blog post, we will discuss the differences between buy limit and stop order so that you can make an informed decision when placing your next stock order.

What is a Buy Limit Order?

A buy limit order is an order placed with a broker to purchase a stock at or below a specified price. This type of order is typically used when an investor wants to purchase a stock at a lower price than its current market value. For example, if you wanted to purchase shares of XYZ Corporation at $50 per share but the current market price was $55 per share, you could place a buy limit order for $50 per share. If the stock drops to or below $50 per share, your order will be executed and you will purchase the shares at that price.

It is important to note that there is no guarantee that your buy limit order will be filled as the stock may never drop to or below your specified price. Additionally, if the stock does drop to or below your specified price but then quickly rises again before your order can be filled, you may miss out on purchasing the shares at that lower price.

What is a Stop Order?

A stop order is an order placed with a broker to purchase or sell a stock once it reaches a certain price point. This type of order is typically used when an investor wants to protect themselves from losses by setting an upper or lower limit on their investment. For example, if you purchased shares of XYZ Corporation at $50 per share but wanted to protect yourself from losses if the stock dropped too low, you could place a stop loss order for $45 per share. If the stock drops to or below $45 per share, your stop loss order will be executed and you will sell your shares at that price in an effort to minimize losses.

It is important to note that there is no guarantee that your stop loss order will be filled as the stock may never reach your specified price point before rising again. Additionally, if the stock does reach your specified price point but then quickly drops again before your order can be filled, you may miss out on selling your shares at that higher price point and instead have them sold for less than what they were worth previously.
