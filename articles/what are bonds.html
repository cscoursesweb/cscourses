Inflation risk is an important concept to consider when investing in bonds because it refers to the potential for changes in price levels, which could lead to a decrease in the value of your bond if inflation rises while your bond's coupon rate remains fixed. Inflation erodes the purchasing power of your money over time, and if your bond's interest rate is not keeping pace with inflation, you could end up losing money in real terms even if you receive interest payments.

For example, suppose you purchase a bond with a fixed annual coupon rate of 3%, and inflation rises from 1% to 3%. In this case, the real interest rate on your bond falls from 2% to 0%, which means you're no longer earning a positive return in real terms. If you were to sell the bond under these conditions, you would likely receive less than what you originally paid for it, which would result in a capital loss.

To mitigate inflation risk, you can consider investing in inflation-protected securities, such as Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS), which adjust their principal value and interest payments based on changes in the Consumer Price Index (CPI). Alternatively, you can diversify your bond portfolio by investing in bonds with varying maturities and credit ratings to spread out your risks.
