Understanding Sequence Risk And How Trend Following Can Help Reduce It

Introduction

Sequence risk is a term used to describe the risk of experiencing a series of losses in a row. It is an important concept for investors to understand, as it can have a significant impact on their portfolios. In this blog post, we will explore what sequence risk is, how it can affect investors, and how trend following strategies can help reduce it.

What Is Sequence Risk?

Sequence risk is the risk of experiencing a series of losses in a row. This type of risk is particularly relevant for investors who are investing in volatile markets or those who are using strategies that involve frequent trading. For example, if an investor buys stocks and then experiences several consecutive days of losses, they may be at greater risk of suffering further losses due to the momentum effect.

Sequence risk can also be referred to as “sequence-of-returns” or “sequence-of-events” risk. It is important to note that sequence risk does not refer to the overall performance of an investment portfolio over time; rather, it refers to the order in which returns are experienced. For example, if an investor experiences two years of positive returns followed by two years of negative returns, they may still have achieved their desired return over the four-year period; however, they may have experienced more volatility than expected due to the order in which those returns were experienced.

How Can Sequence Risk Affect Investors?

Sequence risk can have a significant impact on investors’ portfolios and their ability to achieve their desired return over time. If an investor experiences several consecutive losses early on in their investment journey, they may be at greater risk of suffering further losses due to the momentum effect mentioned above. This could lead them to make decisions that are not in line with their long-term goals or objectives and could potentially result in them missing out on potential gains later on down the line.

In addition, sequence risk can also lead investors to become overly cautious when investing or trading due to fear of further losses. This could lead them to miss out on potential opportunities or make decisions that are not based on sound analysis or research but rather based solely on fear and emotion.

Can Trend Following Help Reduce Sequence Risk?

Trend following strategies can be used by investors as a way to reduce sequence risk and potentially improve their overall performance over time. Trend following involves identifying trends in markets and then taking advantage of those trends by buying assets when prices are rising and selling them when prices are falling (or vice versa). By doing this, investors can potentially benefit from both rising and falling markets while reducing their exposure to sequence risk as they will not be exposed for long periods during periods where prices remain stagnant or move sideways for extended periods of time.

In addition, trend following strategies also allow investors to take advantage of market volatility without having to predict where prices will go next; instead they simply follow existing trends until they change direction (at which point they will switch direction). This means that even if an investor experiences several consecutive losses early on in their investment journey (due to sequence risk), they should still be able to benefit from any subsequent gains as long as they continue following existing trends until those trends change direction again (which could happen at any point).

Finally, trend following strategies also allow investors more flexibility when it comes to managing their portfolios; instead of having all their eggs in one basket (i.e., one particular asset class), trend followers can spread out their investments across multiple asset classes which reduces overall portfolio volatility while still allowing them access potential gains from different markets simultaneously.

Conclusion

Sequence risk is an important concept for investors to understand as it can have a significant impact on their portfolios over time if left unchecked or unmanaged properly.
