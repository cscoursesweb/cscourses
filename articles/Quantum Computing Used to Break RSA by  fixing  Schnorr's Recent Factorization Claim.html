Quantum Computing Used to Break RSA by "fixing" Schnorr's Recent Factorization Claim?

Quantum computing has been a hot topic in the tech world for some time now, and its potential applications are vast. One of the most exciting possibilities is the potential to break RSA, the most widely used public-key cryptography system. Recently, a team of researchers from the University of Bristol and the University of Bristol's Centre for Quantum Technologies have claimed to have broken RSA using quantum computing.


The team used a technique called “Schnorr’s factorization claim” to break RSA. This technique is based on the fact that RSA relies on the difficulty of factoring large numbers. By using a quantum computer, the team was able to factor large numbers much faster than is possible with traditional computers. This allowed them to break RSA and decrypt messages that were encrypted using the system.


The team’s breakthrough is significant because it shows that quantum computing can be used to break RSA. This means that any system that relies on RSA for security could be vulnerable to attack. However, the team was quick to point out that their breakthrough does not mean that RSA is now obsolete.


The team also noted that their breakthrough does not mean that quantum computing can be used to break all encryption systems. In fact, the team believes that their breakthrough could actually be used to improve the security of RSA. They suggest that by “fixing” Schnorr’s factorization claim, it could be possible to make RSA more secure against quantum computing attacks.
