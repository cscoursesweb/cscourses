Why is AES ciphertext entropy always close to 8?

The Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) is a widely used symmetric encryption algorithm that is used to protect sensitive data. It is a block cipher, meaning it takes a fixed-length block of plaintext as input and produces a fixed-length block of ciphertext as output. AES is considered to be one of the most secure encryption algorithms available today.


One of the key features of AES is its high level of entropy. Entropy is a measure of the randomness of a ciphertext, and it is important for ensuring that the ciphertext is difficult to crack. AES has a particularly high level of entropy, and it is always close to 8.


The reason why AES ciphertext entropy is always close to 8 is because of the way the algorithm works. This means that the ciphertext is always the same length as the plaintext, and the entropy of the ciphertext is determined by the length of the plaintext.


AES uses a 128-bit block size, meaning that the plaintext is always 128 bits in length. This means that the entropy of the ciphertext is always close to 8, since 128 bits is equal to 8 bytes. This is because each byte of the ciphertext is determined by 8 bits of the plaintext.
