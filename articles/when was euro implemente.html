The Origins of the Euro

The idea for a single European currency dates back to 1969 when a report by a committee chaired by French economist Robert Marjolin proposed that all European countries should adopt a single currency. This proposal was met with resistance from some countries, but in 1979, five EU member states (Belgium, France, Germany, Italy and Luxembourg) signed an agreement to create a single European currency known as the “European Currency Unit” (ECU). This agreement was later expanded to include all EU member states except Denmark and the United Kingdom.

In 1989, Jacques Delors, then President of the European Commission, proposed that all EU member states should adopt a single currency by 1999. This proposal was met with resistance from some countries who were concerned about losing their national sovereignty over monetary policy. However, in 1992 at a summit in Maastricht (Netherlands), 12 EU member states agreed to form an economic and monetary union (EMU) by 1999. This agreement laid out specific criteria that each country had to meet before they could join EMU such as low inflation rates and budget deficits.

The Introduction of Euro Notes and Coins

In 1998, 11 EU member states met in Brussels to sign an agreement which established a three-year transition period for introducing euro notes and coins into circulation. On 1 January 2002, euro notes and coins were officially introduced into circulation in 12 EU countries (Austria, Belgium, Finland, France, Germany, Greece Ireland Italy Luxembourg Netherlands Portugal Spain). The remaining seven EU countries adopted the euro on 1 January 2007 (Cyprus Malta Slovakia Slovenia Estonia Latvia Lithuania).
