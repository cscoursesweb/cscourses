How to relate the definition for entailment, with soundness and completeness?

Entailment, soundness, and completeness are all important concepts in logic and mathematics. Entailment is the relationship between two statements, where one statement logically follows from the other. Soundness and completeness are two properties of a logical system that determine how well it can be used to make valid deductions.


Entailment is the relationship between two statements, where one statement logically follows from the other. For example, if statement A is true, then statement B must also be true. This is known as the principle of entailment. Entailment is a fundamental concept in logic and mathematics, and is used to determine the validity of arguments and proofs.


Soundness is a property of a logical system that determines how well it can be used to make valid deductions. A logical system is said to be sound if all of its valid deductions are also true. In other words, a sound system will never lead to a false conclusion.


Completeness is another property of a logical system that determines how well it can be used to make valid deductions. A logical system is said to be complete if all of its valid deductions can be derived from the axioms of the system. In other words, a complete system will never lead to an invalid conclusion.
