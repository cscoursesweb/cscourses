What to put in header copyright notice after total conversion of library that didn't have license headers?

When it comes to copyright notices, it can be difficult to know what to put in the header after a total conversion of a library that didn’t have license headers. Copyright notices are important for protecting the intellectual property of the creator of the library, and it’s important to make sure that the copyright notice is accurate and up-to-date.


The first step in determining what to put in the header copyright notice is to determine who owns the copyright to the library. If the library was created by an individual, then the individual owns the copyright. If the library was created by a company, then the company owns the copyright. Once the copyright owner is determined, the copyright notice should include the name of the copyright owner, the year the library was created, and a statement that the library is protected by copyright law.


The next step is to determine what type of license the library is under. If the library is open source, then the copyright notice should include the name of the open source license, such as the GNU General Public License (GPL). If the library is proprietary, then the copyright notice should include a statement that the library is proprietary and all rights are reserved.


Finally, the copyright notice should include a statement that the library is provided “as is” and without any warranties or guarantees. This statement is important to protect the copyright owner from any liability that may arise from the use of the library.
