Will XORing two bad hashes lead to a collision resistant hash?

The short answer to the question of whether XORing two bad hashes will lead to a collision resistant hash is no. XORing two bad hashes will not lead to a collision resistant hash.


A hash is a mathematical algorithm that takes an input of any size and produces an output of a fixed size. The output is known as a hash value or a digest. The purpose of a hash is to provide a unique representation of the input data. A hash is considered to be collision resistant if it is difficult to find two different inputs that produce the same output.


XORing is a logical operation that takes two binary values and produces a single output. It is commonly used in cryptography to combine two values into a single value. XORing two bad hashes will not lead to a collision resistant hash because the output of the XOR operation will still be a bad hash.


The reason why XORing two bad hashes will not lead to a collision resistant hash is because the XOR operation does not change the underlying structure of the hashes. The XOR operation simply combines the two hashes into a single value. The underlying structure of the hashes remains the same, which means that the output of the XOR operation will still be a bad hash.


In order to create a collision resistant hash, the underlying structure of the hashes must be changed. This can be done by using a cryptographic hash function such as SHA-256 or SHA-512. Cryptographic hash functions are designed to be collision resistant and are widely used in cryptography.
